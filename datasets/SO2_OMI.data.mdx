      ## Introduction
      <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.4em' }}>
      Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) is a significant air pollutant that affects human health, air quality, and climate, with exposure linked to respiratory issues and cardiovascular diseases. It is primarily emitted from volcanic eruptions, industrial processes (such as burning fossil fuels), and shipping activities. Monitoring SO₂ is crucial for understanding volcanic activity, tracking industrial emissions, and assessing the impact of shipping on air quality.
      </p>
      <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.4em' }}>
      The Ozone Monitoring Instrument (OMI) aboard NASA’s Aura satellite provides daily, global measurements of SO₂ concentrations in Dobson Units (DU), offering a consistent and reliable data record that supports research and policymaking. OMI measures not only SO₂ but also other criteria pollutants such as ozone (O₃), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), and aerosols, enabling detailed analysis of pollution sources and trends. Building on the legacy of NASA’s Total Ozone Mapping Spectrometer (TOMS) and the European Space Agency’s Global Ozone Monitoring Experiment (GOME), OMI offers improved ground resolution and the ability to detect a wider range of atmospheric constituents, including lower atmospheric dust, smoke, and volcanic plumes, making it a vital tool for air quality and climate research.
      </p>
      <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.4em' }}>
        Monthly SO₂ data can be used to identify and monitor volcanic eruptions, track SO₂ emissions from industrial facilities, and assess the impact of new regulations on shipping emissions. Analysis of OMI data has revealed significant SO₂ plumes from both natural and anthropogenic sources around the world.
      </p>
    </p>
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block style={{justifyContent: 'center', alignItems: 'center', margin: '0 auto', width: '100%' }}>

      <p>
    ## Data Preparation
        The dataset undergoes comprehensive processing to ensure accuracy, beginning with adjustments for cloud cover and surface reflectivity. The SO₂ algorithm employs spectral fitting in the UV range via the Differential Optical Absorption Spectroscopy (DOAS) technique to retrieve SO₂ vertical column densities. These values are then converted and reported in Dobson Units (DU). Ground-based measurements and independent satellite observations provide SO₂ estimates to further validate OMI satellite observations.
    </p>
  </Prose>
</Block>

    ## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)
    <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.7em' }}>
      <strong>What is SO₂?</strong><br />
      SO₂ is a harmful air pollutant primarily emitted from volcanic eruptions and the burning of fossil fuels. It contributes to respiratory issues and environmental degradation, including acid rain.
    </p>
    <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.7em' }}>
      <strong>How does sulfur dioxide interact with other air pollutants?</strong><br />
      Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) can react with other compounds in the atmosphere to form sulfate aerosols, which contribute to respiratory problems and can affect climate by reflecting sunlight.  It also combines with water vapor to create acid rain, worsening environmental damage.
    </p>
    <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.7em' }}>
      <strong>How can I use this dataset?</strong><br />
      Applications include volcanic eruption monitoring, industrial emission tracking, shipping lane monitoring, air quality monitoring, and climate modeling.
    </p>
    <p style={{ marginBottom: '0.7em' }}>
      <strong>What is a Dobson Unit (DU)?</strong><br />
      The Dobson Unit (DU) is a unit of measurement for the total amount of a trace gas, like SO₂, in a vertical column of the Earth's atmosphere. One DU is defined as 0.01 mm thickness of the gas if it were compressed to standard temperature and pressure.
    </p>
