    ## Monitoring Methane Emissions from Space: A Satellite Overview
  </Prose>

<Block>

    ### Introduction

Picture the Earth from above, a blue and green marble speckled with clouds and teeming with life. Yet, unseen to the naked eye, a potent greenhouse gas—methane—seeps into the atmosphere from various sources, contributing significantly to global warming. Scientists are harnessing the power of satellites to pinpoint and quantify these emissions across the globe.

    Open with breathtaking clips of Earth from space, transitioning to time-lapsed imagery of sprawling forests, bustling oil fields, and wetlands. Methane is responsible for approximately **0.6°C** of global warming, significantly impacting our climate. Satellite observations, such as those from GOSAT and TROPOMI, are becoming pivotal tools in managing and mitigating these emissions.

        ### The Satellite Fleet

High above the Earth, a fleet of satellites carries sophisticated instruments designed to sniff out methane at various altitudes and resolutions. Animation of satellites in orbit highlights instruments like the SWIR sensors on board TROPOMI and GOSAT.

    The ongoing revolution in satellite technology allows us to observe methane emissions globally—from large-scale maps to pinpointing specific industrial leaks or natural sources with **unprecedented accuracy**.
      </Prose>
    </Block>

    <Block>
      <Prose>
        ### Current and Future Capabilities

Fast forward to 2023 and beyond, envisioning an enhanced global network of advanced satellite missions aiming for higher resolutions and faster revisit times. Renderings of next-generation satellites such as MethaneSAT and Sentinel-5 on the launchpad and in orbit are showcased.

    As technology advances, so does our capability to monitor methane emissions with increasing precision. This enables countries to meet climate pledges like the **Paris Agreement** and the **Global Methane Pledge**, which aim to reduce emissions significantly by **2030**.
      </Prose>
    </Block>

        ### Challenges and Opportunities

Pan over varied terrains: the complex wetlands, busy oil and gas fields, and remote Arctic regions where methane emissions are most challenging to track. Footage shows both the beauty and challenges of monitoring methane in adverse environments—cloud-capped forests, reflective water bodies, and polar darkness.

    Despite advances, hurdles remain. **Cloud cover**, water's reflectivity, and equipment limitations create challenges for full atmospheric methane monitoring. Yet, solving these hurdles with innovative technologies could vastly improve understanding and strategic reductions in emissions.
